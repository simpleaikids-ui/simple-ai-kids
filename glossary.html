Glossary

AI words explained in plain language. Each one has an example too.

AI (Artificial Intelligence)

Computer programs that can do tasks that usually need a human brain — like recognizing pictures, writing sentences, or playing games. Example: The feature that unlocks your phone by seeing your face.

Model

The "brain" that a computer builds from lots of examples. It's what makes the guesses. Example: After you show the computer 30 photos of apples and 30 of bananas, the thing that learned to tell them apart is the model.

Training Data

The examples you show a model to help it learn. More variety usually means a smarter model. Example: The 30 apple photos and 30 banana photos you took.

Bias

When a model only knows one kind of example, it gets things wrong for other kinds. Bias comes from unfair training data. Example: If you only show the computer red apples, it might not recognize a green one.

Neural Network

A kind of model made of lots of little math pieces connected together, loosely inspired by how brain cells connect. Most modern AI uses neural networks.

Prompt

The question or instruction you give to a chatbot. Better prompts usually get better answers. Example: "Write a story about a dragon who can't breathe fire" is a prompt.

LLM (Large Language Model)

A very big AI model trained on tons of text — what powers chatbots like Claude and ChatGPT.

Embedding

A way to turn words or pictures into a list of numbers that captures their meaning. Two things with similar meanings get similar numbers. Example: "happy" and "joyful" would have close numbers; "happy" and "banana" would be far apart.

Hallucination

When AI confidently makes up something that isn't true. Always double-check important facts from chatbots.

Fine-Tuning

Taking a model that already knows a lot, and training it a little more on a specific topic so it gets extra good at that one thing.

API

A way for one program to talk to another. When your project "calls an API," it's asking a big AI service to do something and send back the answer.

Overfitting

When a model memorizes its training data instead of actually learning the pattern. It does great on old examples but fails on new ones.
