For Grown-Ups

A quick guide for parents and teachers — what's safe, what's needed, and how to help when kids get stuck.

Our Approach

This site exists because most "AI for kids" material explains AI without letting kids actually build it. Our projects flip that: every one produces something the kid makes with their own hands (well, their own clicks). Projects are organized by age so a 6-year-old and a 16-year-old both find something real to do.

What You'll Need at Home

For the 5–8 and 9–12 bands, a Chromebook, laptop, or iPad with a browser is enough. Some projects use the webcam. A few use microphones. No installs, no paid accounts, no credit card.

For 13–15, kids use Google Colab (free, browser-based Python). A free Google account is required.

For 16+, projects vary. Most run in the browser; a few benefit from a local Python setup. A handful use paid AI APIs — typically under $5 in credits completes a project. We flag these clearly on the project page.

Data & Privacy

We choose tools that keep student data local or research-only wherever possible:

  • Teachable Machine — webcam processing happens in the browser; images don't leave the device unless the user exports a model.
  • Machine Learning for Kids — designed for schools; student data stays inside the platform.
  • Scratch — no account needed for local projects.
  • Google Colab — runs code on Google's servers under your Google account.
  • AI APIs (OpenAI, Anthropic, etc.) — text sent to these services is governed by their privacy policies. We recommend not sending personal info through chat projects.

Start Fresh

Every kid's progress is stored only on this device — nothing is sent to us. If you want to replay a single project with a sibling or a student, each project page now has its own ↺ Start this project over button next to the progress bar. It clears just that project's step checkmarks, sticker, and saved note — nothing else is touched.

When Your Kid Gets Stuck

Every project page has a "If something goes wrong…" callout that covers the most common problems. Beyond that, the best help you can offer is usually just patience: most failures come from too little training data, a typo in code, or a missed step. Scroll back to the last step that clearly worked and go from there.

For Teachers

These projects work well as single class periods (ages 5–12) or multi-day units (13+). A downloadable one-pager per project is on our roadmap. Feedback welcome via the contact link below.

Talking About AI With Kids

A few ideas worth reinforcing:

  • AI learns from examples. The examples it sees shape what it knows — and what it gets wrong.
  • AI can be confidently wrong. It's okay to disagree with the computer.
  • AI is made by people. The choices those people make (what data, what goals) matter.

🖨 Printable: The 5-Minute AI Safety Talk

A small script you can read with your kid before they start. Print it, put it on the fridge, revisit as needed.

🧑‍🤝‍🧑 The 5-Minute AI Safety Talk

  1. AI is a guesser, not a knower. It learned from examples. If the examples were wrong, AI will be wrong too.
  2. Don't share secrets with a chatbot. No real names, addresses, school names, phone numbers, or passwords — not even as a joke.
  3. AI can sound confident and still be wrong. If something feels off, check with a grown-up or a trusted source.
  4. Pictures and voices can be faked. If a video or voice seems weird or surprising, pause. Ask first.
  5. You are in charge. If an AI answer makes you feel sad, scared, or confused, close the tab and tell a grown-up.
  6. Being kind still matters. Even to a chatbot. What we practice online, we practice in life.

Family promise: I'll show a grown-up anything I'm unsure about, and I'll never give a computer my full name, address, or photos of my friends.
