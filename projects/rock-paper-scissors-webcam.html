Rock-Paper-Scissors Webcam

Train your webcam to see ✊ ✋ ✌️ — then hook it up to Scratch and play rock-paper-scissors against the computer!

What You'll Make

A working rock-paper-scissors game where you play with your real hand, not buttons. The computer sees your move and picks one of its own. First to 5 wins!

What You'll Need

  • A computer with a webcam
  • A free Machine Learning for Kids account (a grown-up sets this up)
  • About 45 minutes

What You'll Learn

  • How to train an image classifier
  • How the number of examples changes the model's accuracy
  • How to wire a trained model into a Scratch game
🧑‍🍳 Grown-Up Notes ML for Kids by Dale Lane is free and designed for classrooms. Student data stays inside the platform. You'll create a teacher account, then give the student a class code. Budget 10 extra minutes for first-time setup.

Steps

  1. Log into Machine Learning for Kids and create a new Image project called RPS.
  2. Click Train. Create three buckets: rock, paper, scissors.
  3. Use the webcam to add ~30 images per bucket. For each pose, move your hand left, right, up, and down. Show it at different distances.
  4. Add a fourth bucket: nothing. Take 30 photos of an empty background or your face. This teaches the model what "no move" looks like.
  5. Click Learn & Test → Train new machine learning model. Wait ~30 seconds.
  6. Test it! Show each pose — the confidence bar should jump to the right bucket. If not, add more examples.
  7. Click Make → Scratch 3. A Scratch window opens with blocks for your model.
  8. Start a new Scratch project. Add this logic: 
    when green flag clicked
forever
  set [move v] to (recognise image (costume #) as label)
  if <(move) = [rock]> then say [I saw rock! Picking paper 📄] for 2 secs
  if <(move) = [paper]> then say [I saw paper! Picking scissors ✂️] for 2 secs
  if <(move) = [scissors]> then say [I saw scissors! Picking rock ✊] for 2 secs
    (The exact blocks depend on the ML4K Scratch extension — the recognise image block will appear once you connect your project.)
  9. Keep score! Add two variables: you and computer. Update them using rock-paper-scissors rules.
  10. Play 5 rounds. Who wins?
🪄 If something goes wrong… The model picks "rock" no matter what? Check if your hand fills enough of the frame — zoom in. Are lighting conditions different now than when you trained? Retrain with examples in your current lighting.

Try This Next

  • Add Lizard and Spock. Expand to Rock-Paper-Scissors-Lizard-Spock. Add two more gestures and the win/loss logic.
  • Build a fair computer. Instead of always beating you, make the computer pick randomly. Who wins more?
  • Best of 7. Add a "who won the match" screen after 7 rounds.
