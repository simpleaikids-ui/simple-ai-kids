Voice-Controlled Maze

Yell "up," "down," "left," and "right" to guide a character through a maze. Build a voice-recognition model and wire it to Scratch.

What You'll Make

A maze game where the player can't touch the keyboard. They shout directions and the character moves. Works best with 2 players — one speaks, one cheers.

What You'll Need

  • A computer with a microphone
  • A quiet-ish room
  • Teachable Machine (audio project) or Machine Learning for Kids
  • About 60 minutes

What You'll Learn

  • How audio models are different from image models (they listen in chunks)
  • Why background noise matters
  • How to build a simple maze in Scratch
🧑‍🍳 Grown-Up Notes Audio projects are sensitive to noise. Pick a quiet time. Younger siblings in the background will get into the training data and confuse the model later. For Teachable Machine Audio, Chrome or Edge is required.

Steps

  1. Go to teachablemachine.withgoogle.com. Click Audio Project.
  2. Record 20 seconds of Background Noise (required — this is the "quiet" class).
  3. Add four classes: up, down, left, right. For each, click record and say the word — then repeat it in slightly different tones — at least 20 times per class. Teachable Machine will show you samples.
  4. Click Train Model. Test by saying the words. The bars should jump correctly.
  5. Click Export Model → copy the sharable link (keeps the model hosted by Google).
  6. Open Scratch via Machine Learning for Kids with the same classes, or use the Teachable Machine Scratch extension in Scratch Lab (lab.scratch.mit.edu).
  7. Draw a maze: use the pencil tool to draw walls on the stage. Add a sprite as the player character.
  8. Build the movement logic: 
    when green flag clicked
go to x: (-200) y: (-150)
forever
  set [cmd v] to (recognise audio as label)
  if <(cmd) = [up]>    then change y by 10
  if <(cmd) = [down]>  then change y by -10
  if <(cmd) = [left]>  then change x by -10
  if <(cmd) = [right]> then change x by 10
  if <touching [maze wall v]?> then say [Ouch!] for 0.5 secs > go to x: (-200) y: (-150)
  9. Play! Shout your way to the finish line.
🪄 If something goes wrong… "left" and "right" keep getting confused? Their sounds are actually similar. Record more examples. Try louder, clearer pronunciations. Or pick words that sound more different — "west" and "east."

Try This Next

  • Timer. Add a timer. How fast can you finish?
  • Two-player relay. One player says the moves, the other has to keep their hands behind their back.
  • Language experiment. Train it in a different language. Does it still work if you mix languages?
