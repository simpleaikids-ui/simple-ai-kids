Talk to a Robot

Chat with a super-smart robot and try to trick it with silly questions. You'll discover that robots are clever — but also sometimes very, very wrong.

What You'll Make

You'll have a conversation with an AI chatbot (like Claude, ChatGPT, or Gemini). You'll ask it silly questions, serious questions, and tricky questions — and draw what you learn in a little "robot report."

What You'll Need

  • A computer with a browser
  • A grown-up to open the chatbot for you (most chatbots require users to be 13+)
  • Paper and crayons for your robot report
  • About 15 minutes

What You'll Learn

  • That AI chatbots are trained on lots of books and websites
  • That chatbots can be wrong even when they sound very sure
  • How asking a question the right way gets better answers — that's called a prompt
🧑‍🍳 Grown-Up Notes Most consumer chatbots require users to be 13 or 18+. For 5–8, the grown-up types on behalf of the child. Stick to general-knowledge questions and avoid any personal info. Sit together — it's a great teaching moment about trust and verification. You can also use kid-focused options like Khan Academy's Khanmigo (with parental account).

Steps

  1. Ask your grown-up to open a chatbot like claude.ai, chatgpt.com, or gemini.google.com. They'll log in for you.
  2. Think of a question. Start easy: "Why is the sky blue?" Tell your grown-up to type it in.
  3. Read the answer together. Does it make sense? Did you learn something new?
  4. Now try a silly one: "If a pig could fly, where would it go?" See what it writes!
  5. Try a trick question: ask "How many legs does a spider with three heads have?" Spiders don't have three heads — does the robot notice?
  6. Try asking the same question in a different way. Example: "Tell me about dinosaurs" vs "Explain dinosaurs like I'm 5." Do you get different answers?
  7. On your paper, draw the robot and write two things: one cool thing it got right, and one silly thing it got wrong.
🪄 If something goes wrong… Does the robot refuse to answer? Some questions are marked as "I shouldn't answer that." That's okay — the robot has rules, just like you have rules at school. Pick a different question!

Try This Next

  • Ask it to write a poem. Tell it to write a poem about your favorite animal. Change one word — does the poem change too?
  • Ask the same question twice. Close the window and ask again. Does it say the exact same thing? (Usually not!)
  • Fact check it. Pick a fact from the robot's answer. Look it up in a book or ask your grown-up. Is it right?
