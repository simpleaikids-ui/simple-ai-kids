AI Fortune Teller

Build a silly fortune-telling chatbot — then investigate how the examples you give it change what it predicts. A lesson in bias, disguised as magic!

What You'll Make

A fortune-telling chatbot that classifies your mood from a sentence and spits out a silly prediction. You'll train two versions: one with cheerful examples, one with grumpy ones — and see how the same input gets opposite fortunes.

What You'll Need

  • A computer with a browser
  • A free Machine Learning for Kids account
  • About 45 minutes
  • A notebook to plan example sentences

What You'll Learn

  • What bias in training data looks like — concretely
  • How to label sentences and train a text classifier
  • How your choices shape what an AI says
🧑‍🍳 Grown-Up Notes Great bias/ethics teaching moment. After the kid trains the grumpy version, discuss: does this look like anything they've seen online (social feeds, recommendations)? The point isn't that AI is bad — it's that humans' choices shape it.

Steps

  1. Create a new Text project called Fortune in ML for Kids.
  2. Add two labels: happy and sad.
  3. Version 1 (balanced): add 10 happy-sounding sentences and 10 sad-sounding sentences. Examples: "I'm excited about tomorrow" (happy), "My cat is sick" (sad).
  4. Train the model. Test: type "I'm not sure how I feel." What does it predict?
  5. Open Scratch via ML4K. Wire up: 
    when green flag clicked
ask [How are you feeling today?] and wait
set [mood v] to (recognise text (answer) as label)
if <(mood) = [happy]> then say [Your fortune: Good pizza awaits! 🍕] for 3 secs
if <(mood) = [sad]> then say [Your fortune: Extra hugs incoming 🤗] for 3 secs
  6. Save and test the fortune teller with 3 friends or family.
  7. Version 2 (biased): make a second project. Now put 10 happy sentences under happy… but also 10 grumpy-leaning sentences under happy (things like "Fine, I guess" or "Whatever"). The sad label only has very extreme sadness.
  8. Train. Test: "I had a great day!" — does it still say happy? Try "Fine, I guess." Then try neutral things.
  9. Write in your notebook: Did the second fortune teller give different predictions? Why?
🪄 If something goes wrong… Both versions give the same prediction? Your examples might not be different enough. Version 2 should have clearly ambiguous sentences under "happy" so the model's sense of "happy" gets warped.

Try This Next

  • Add a third mood. Introduce sleepy with its own examples and fortune.
  • Break it on purpose. Feed the model sentences in a new topic (food, weather). How does it do?
  • Ethics moment. Write 3 sentences about how biased training data could hurt people in the real world.
